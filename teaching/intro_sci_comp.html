Experimental course for Fall 2014: Introduction to Scientific Computing

Computation has become integral to engineering, science, commerce, politics, humanities, and more. Most of this computation is in the form of the manipulation of numbers, not symbolic expressions (as in most math) or text and other discrete data types (as in most computer science).

The purpose of this course, Introduction to scientific computing, is to provide you with an entry point into this young science. A successful student will learn valuable tools for future research and employment opportunities. More than that, you will learn a way quantifying the world that is complementary to the classical methods of calculus and abstract manipulation.

Prerequisite
MATH 242 or equivalent placement by the department. If you want to take this course concurrently with 242, see me.
Topics covered
(still under construction) The plan is to cover descriptive statistics of data, harmonic analysis of signals, fitting functions to data, linear difference equations (recurrence relations), continuous dynamics (ordinary differential equations), and coding techniques such as validation, testing, and source control.
Applications

The course will be taught within the context of contemporary applications of computing. Some of these are drawn from current research projects. Examples include: analysis of human vital signs data, transformations of music and images, experimental data on human eyes, simple financial modeling and forecasting, and swarming and flocking behavior. Class interests may inform what will be covered.

However, while the applications will be kept somewhat realistic, they are motivational and illustrative. The focus of the course is on the underlying mathematical and computational tools that apply in a wide variety of contexts.

Resources
The course will be taught with regular if not daily use of MATLAB, the most dominant environment for desktop scientific computing. It is strongly recommended that you have a reasonable personal laptop to use for the class. If that is impossible for you, see me. You will also be required to buy a student version of MATLAB, which is about $100, and valid for as long as you are a student. (There will be no textbook purchase required.)
Pedogogical style
The course will emphasize an active learning style. In other words, it will not be a traditional lecture/homework course, but resemble a 'flipped classroom.' This means that you will learn much of the content online outside of class meeting time, and regular attendance and energetic participation in class will be expected.
Experimental course??

For Fall 2014 this course will be given the number MATH 267, which is an 'experimental course.' This means that the department is evaluating it as a more permanent addition. It also means the course may be rougher around the edges than one of the old standbys.

Because it will be at the 200 level, it might not count for elective credit in many majors and minors. See your academic adviser if you have questions. For Math B.S. majors, this course will be accepted as an alternative to CISC 220, though it will not be accepted as a substitute for advanced courses which require CISC 220 as a prerequisite.
