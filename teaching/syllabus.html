Textbook and notes

The textbook for the course is Numerical Analysis, 7th ed., by Burden and Faires. However, the course will not follow the book closely in every regard. There is also a set of notes written by me that you are required to buy from the bookstore. (No, I don't get a kickback.) The notes should be considered the primary source material. Some of the material in the notes does not appear in the text.

Due dates

Assignments are due by 5 PM on the due date. These dates are not negotiable. Late work will not be accepted without a valid excuse.

Academic integrity

You are expected and encouraged to discuss problems with others. However, the work you turn in must be completely your own.

For example, fixing an error in one line of a classmate's code is no big deal. Emailing your entire solution to him is a big deal and is a violation of the integrity policy. So is working side-by-side and turning in the same code with some variable names changed.

Sharing or copying will result in penalties for all involved parties. A first incident will typically result in a zero score; a second incident will probably result in failing the course and a referral for disciplinary action.

Homework

Homework will be collected on Wednesdays (not necessarily every week). Expect the homework assignments to be time-consuming. They are a major component of the course and your grade.

MATLAB

Learning and using the software package MATLAB is a part of the course. The course notes have an appendix with a basic guide to MATLAB. This should be enough to get you through the class. If you want a more complete reference, I recommend Getting Started with MATLAB by R. Pratap. You can use MATLAB in certain university labs, run it on the UNIX mainframes, or purchase a student version for about \$100 from mathworks.com. For more information, visit the web site.

Exams

There will be two pencil-and-paper midterm exams on nonoverlapping units of the course.

Projects

There will be two projects throughout the term, including one due during the finals period. They will test your ability to solve more complex computer problems than are found on the homework.
