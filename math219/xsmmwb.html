Introduction¶

This dataset was retrieved from the publicly available data from the U.S. government, and it was published by the U.S Fish and Wildlife Service. It summarizes water quality data that was obtained from five different pools of water located in the Back Bay National Wildlife Refuge in Virginia. The data was collected approximately every two weeks for 30 years, and it was recorded with the intent to protect the aquatic organisms found in this refuge.

Preprocessing¶

This data was found all in one dataset, so no merging was performed between frames. To prepare this data, I began by reviewing the columns and removing those that were of little value to my analysis. Also, I decided to index the frame by the date the data was recorded as this makes the data more natural to read.

The last step of preprocessing the data involved identifying any outlier values in the quantitative columns. This will help me discover any inconsistencies that may have occurred during the measuring of this data. I decided to group the data by the five different pools of water as the results of one pool should not impact the results of another. I did not the want to calculate outliers that created misconceptions of the actual data as the five pools are located in five different areas of the refuge. Note that if a pool does not appear in the list of outliers, then that pool does not have any outliers for that measurement.

by_pool = water_quality.groupby("Site_Id")

def is_outlier(x):
    Q25, Q75 = x.quantile([.25,.75])
    I = Q75 - Q25
    return (x < Q25 - 1.5*I) |  (x > Q75 + 1.5*I)

The rest of the columns do display some exciting results. The salinity of the bay pool is much higher and has a wider distribution than the other four pools because they are intended to be freshwater while the bay is the only pool that is directly connected to the Atlantic Ocean. The other four pools are secluded, so this verifies why the measurements are clustered towards the lower salinity levels in the salinity graph. Additionally, given the size of the bay, it averages more dissolved oxygen because it has a larger surface area to collect the oxygen from the air compared to the smaller pools. The graph of the bay's dissolved oxygen levels resembles a normal distribtion, which supports the consistency in the recorded measurements. However, freshwater is better than salt water at absorbing dissolved oxygen, so this helps explain why the dissolved oxygen average for the bay is not too much higher than the other pools. The D-pool acting as a fishing pond can also help explain why the turbidity, measured with the Secchi disk, is much higher than the other four pools. Not only does fishing cause the fish to become more active and kick up the dirt, but also, fishermen are allowed to wade in the pool or use kayaks, which also can cause mud to be unsettled. At first glance, the maximum Secchi depth of the C-pool appears to standout because it is so low; however, it can be noted that maximum water depth for that pool was also extremely low. Furthermore, the low average pH for the D-pool can initially be viewed as a concern given that the pH scale changes logarithmically, but all five averages lie within the ideal pH for aquatic organisms.
